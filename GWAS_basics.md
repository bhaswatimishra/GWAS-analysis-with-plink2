# Part 1: GWAS Basics

## What is GWAS?

GWAS stands for **Genome-Wide Association Study**.

It is a method used to find genetic variants (SNPs) that may be associated with a disease or trait.

In simple words, GWAS compares **cases** (people with the disease) and **controls** (people without the disease) to see if any SNP is more common in one group.

---

## What is a SNP?

SNP stands for **Single Nucleotide Polymorphism**.

It is a change in a single DNA base.

For example:

* Person 1 has **A**
* Person 2 has **G**

At the same position in DNA, this difference is called a SNP.

---

## Cases and Controls

* **Cases** = individuals who have the disease.
* **Controls** = individuals who do not have the disease.

GWAS checks whether an allele is more frequent in cases than in controls.

---

## Allele Frequency

Allele frequency tells us how common an allele is in a population.

Example:

If 20 copies of allele **G** are present out of 200 total allele copies, then:

**G allele frequency = 20 / 200 = 0.10 = 10%**

---

## Minor Allele Frequency (MAF)

MAF is the frequency of the **less common allele** in a population.

Example:

* A allele frequency = 90%
* G allele frequency = 10%

Here, **MAF = 10%**.

MAF is used to remove very rare variants that may give unreliable results.

---

## Why is GWAS useful?

GWAS helps researchers find **genomic regions** that may be linked to diseases such as diabetes, cancer, heart disease, and many other traits.

---

## Important Point

GWAS shows **association**, not direct **causation**.

This means a SNP may be associated with a disease, but it does not automatically mean that the SNP causes the disease.
